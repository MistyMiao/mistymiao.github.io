Misinformation is often treated as a cognitive failure that should diminish when people attend more carefully to accuracy, but this inattention-based explanation remains incomplete. We advance a coalitional account in which people sometimes share misinformation to (1) coordinate joint attention and action, (2) signal allegiance to allies, and (3) project dominance toward rivals. We tested this account using longitudinal behavioral data from Twitter, analyzing 170,627 tweets from 241 users observed from 2018 to 2022. Misinformation sharing was identified via a retrieval-augmented verification pipeline, and users’ moral-foundation profiles were quantified from their historical tweets using Mformer. Consistent with coalitional predictions, binding foundations—especially authority—positively predicted misinformation sharing, whereas individualizing foundations—especially care—were negatively associated with this behavior. We further examined whether pandemic-related pathogen threat moderated these associations: misinformation sharing increased after the onset of COVID-19, with a steeper rise among users higher in binding concerns. These findings provide converging evidence for the coalitional account and suggest that people share falsehoods not merely because of cognitive error, but also as a functionally adaptive response to coalitional demands under threat.
